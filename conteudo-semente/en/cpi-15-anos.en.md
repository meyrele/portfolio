## About the project

In 2026, Climate Policy Initiative Brazil marks fifteen years as a leading research organization in climate finance and public policy, in partnership with PUC-Rio. I developed the commemorative seal, campaign strategy and anniversary identity system, ahead of an event scheduled for August in Rio de Janeiro.

The project included the seal in multiple application versions, a campaign-specific palette and typographic system, and usage guidelines for light, dark and photographic backgrounds.

## Problem

CPI's audience — international funders, policymakers, researchers — rejects festive language. Balloons, vivid gradients and celebration without substance were out of the question. At the same time, the anniversary could not read as retrospective: the fifteen-year mark had to work as a credibility platform for what comes next, especially the Global Forest Roadmap, developed in cooperation with the COP presidency.

There was also a non-negotiable brand constraint: CPI's official logo cannot be altered. The seal had to coexist with the brand without replacing or competing with it.

## Solution

The right metaphor was not in the number — it was in the forest. A tree stores each year of growth in its rings; CPI accumulated layers of research whose value only appears when seen together. From that insight came the Rings of Time concept: three concentric rings representing the accumulated past, a present of peak relevance and a future under construction. The circular form converses with the spiral in the official logo, creating continuity without imitation.

The process ran through four phases: immersion in brand materials and strategic meeting transcripts; conceptual work along four axes (connection, impact, future, forest); exploration of six distinct visual directions, assessed for harmony with the parent brand and versatility; and system consolidation, with a palette anchored in terracotta and charcoal and a serif display voice paired with an institutional sans for body text.

The result is a seal that works as a signature rather than decoration — applicable from technical reports to digital campaign pieces, sustaining the tone the institution built over fifteen years of evidence.
