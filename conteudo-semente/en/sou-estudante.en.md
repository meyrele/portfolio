## About the project

SoU_Estudante turns Brazilian public higher education data — the national Census, Sisu admissions, ENADE quality exams and the e-MEC registry — into practical guidance for people deciding where and what to study. The redesign starts from a platform outdated since 2019 and arrives at an experience centered on the persona Maria Fernanda: the first in her family to attempt university, mobile-only, from the Brazilian countryside.

As design lead, I run the project from research to prototyping: diagnosis of the previous version, international benchmarking (College Scorecard, Discover Uni, GreatSchools), four personas with journey maps, modular architecture, design system and a navigable prototype.

## Problem

Brazil holds rich datasets on higher education, but they reach people fragmented, in technical language and stripped of context — precisely for those who need them most. The previous platform made things worse: data frozen in 2019, no coherent information architecture — data lived on disconnected islands — and an interface unusable on mobile, the primary persona's only access point.

Around three thousand discontinued courses also polluted the system, and no figure disclosed its source.

## Solution

I structured the product as a living cartography: five independent modules, each answering one question in the decision journey — discovering courses, understanding Sisu admissions, mapping financial and social support, reading institutional indicators and comparing up to four courses side by side. Students enter through whichever door makes sense; there is no mandatory linear flow.

Interface decisions follow principles agreed with the research team. Transparency: every figure carries its source and year as part of the component, not as a footnote. Honesty: where data is uncertain, the interface says so plainly. No rankings: the comparison module points to the best course per isolated criterion, never an aggregate score that manufactures false certainty. Numerical context: scholarships and quotas appear as a share of incoming students, avoiding distortion between courses of different sizes.

The visual identity separates layers of information through three typographic voices and a palette with one structural anchor and five accents, one per module. The prototype preserves context across modules: a course found in search leads directly to that same course's admission data.
